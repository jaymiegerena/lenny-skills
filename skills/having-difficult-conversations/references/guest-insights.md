## 1. Core Frameworks

### Radical Candor (Kim Scott)
Care personally AND challenge directly at the same time. The 2×2: obnoxious aggression (challenge without care), ruinous empathy (care without challenge), manipulative insincerity (neither). Most people err toward ruinous empathy — withholding feedback to spare feelings ultimately hurts the person.

> "If I didn't fire Bob, I was going to lose all my best performers because not only had it been unfair to Bob not to tell him, I also had been unfair to everyone on the team."

Emphasized by Maya Prohovnik, Claire Butler, Alexander Embiricos, Anneka Gupta, Ebi Atawodi, Kenneth Berger, Boz, Ben Horowitz.

### SBI — Situation, Behavior, Impact (Paige Costello)
Specify the exact time and place (Situation), describe the observable action without judgment (Behavior), share how it affected you or the team (Impact).

> "The situation is on Tuesday in that meeting at 3:00. Behavior, you interrupted me while I was saying this thing. Impact, made me feel like you weren't listening."

### CORE — Context, Observation, Result, nExt step (Kim Scott)
Works for both praise and criticism. Context: in the meeting. Observation: when you said um every third word. Result: it makes you sound stupid. Next step: go to the speech coach.

### NVC — Nonviolent Communication (Rachel Lockett, via Marshall Rosenberg)
Four steps: (1) Observations — objective facts that can't be argued. (2) Feelings — body-based emotions, not "I feel like..." (3) Needs — the underlying human need (clarity, connection, respect). (4) Request — specific and achievable.

> "The goal of any conflict is to create mutual understanding."

### The 4 I's (Matt Abrahams)
Information (set the context), Impact (on you, the giver), Invitation (bring them in to problem-solve), Implications (consequences or benefits of change).

### HHIIPPP (Kim Scott)
Humble, Helpful, Immediate, In-person, Public praise / Private criticism, not Personal. Deliver feedback right away, about work/behavior not personality, synchronously so you can gauge the reaction.

## 2. Performance Feedback & Coaching

### Give feedback immediately and continuously
Feedback should be a daily calibration tool, not a semi-annual event. (Julie Zhuo, Kim Scott, Matt MacInnis)

### Withholding feedback is selfish
"You're optimizing for your own comfort." Reframe difficult feedback as an unselfish act of service. (Matt MacInnis, emphasized by Uri Levine, Kim Scott, Alexander Embiricos, Anneka Gupta, Drew Houston, Ebi Atawodi)

### Design feedback around the intended outcome
Work backward from the behavioral change you want. Trim 90% of emotional fluff — keep only what motivates the change. (Evan LaPointe, Wes Kao)

> Wes Kao: "The goal is behavior change. Trim everything that does not contribute to that goal."

### Use questions and owned observations
"I wonder if you missed an opportunity in that interview" lands better than "You botched that interview." Own it as your perception, not objective truth. (Claire Hughes Johnson)

### Hold people to their potential
Call out when someone chose the convenient path over the right one. Frame it as an expectation of greatness based on respect. (Tobi Lutke)

> "I actually expected better of you... that's hard to hear, because it's right. But it's also extremely valuable."

### Establish feedback-first relationships early
Open with "I want you to help me get better" and ask how they prefer to receive feedback. (Julie Zhuo, Mihika Kapoor)

### Name your own nervousness
"I'm so nervous right now. I want to give you some feedback and I'm worried it'll impact our relationship... but I think it'll help you to hear it." This humanizes the exchange. (Julie Zhuo)

### State your positive intent explicitly
"I care about you and I'm giving you this feedback because I want you to be successful." Makes the recipient far more receptive. (Anneka Gupta, Melissa Tan, Alisa Cohn)

### Frame perception feedback carefully
Sensitive feedback on presence/perception (e.g., how someone's style affects credibility) requires deep trust first. Focus on controllable, actionable elements. (Ada Chen Rekhi)

### Three-step performance feedback
Set clear expectations → give specific feedback on the gap → actively help them close it. Jump in and show what "the bar" looks like. (Elizabeth Stone)

### If gentle feedback doesn't land, increase directness
"When you say um every third word, it makes you sound stupid." Sometimes bluntness is necessary to penetrate defenses. (Kim Scott)

## 3. Firing & Termination

### The Crystal Clear warning must come first
Before firing, explicitly state consequences: "I need you to fix this within 30 days. Otherwise, we're going to part ways." Use the phrase "part ways" or "not have a future together" so there's no ambiguity. (Alisa Cohn)

> "I know you have it in you to change. I value all you bring to the table, but if you don't fix these things, we're not going to have a future together."

### The firing conversation should be brief
Reference previous warnings, state the decision as final, move to logistics. It's a confirmation, not a debate. (Alisa Cohn)

> "We talked about this multiple times. You haven't made the changes. We're going to part ways. Sarah from HR is here to walk through logistics."

### Always deliver in person, 1:1
"The biggest marker between a botched layoff and a successful layoff is whether they heard it from their manager in a one-on-one." Never by email, group chat, or group announcement. (Matt Mochary, emphasized by Sanchan Saxena)

### Separate the decision from the implementation
The business decision may be necessary; the implementation should be compassionate. Become their agent — actively help them find their next job. (Matt Mochary)

### The Keepers Test (Elizabeth Stone)
"If this person said 'I'm leaving,' would I do everything I could to keep them?" If not, initiate the hard conversation now.

### Firing is as important as hiring
Getting good at identifying when someone doesn't belong is a skill. Ask: "If there were no emotions involved, what would I do?" (Molly Graham, emphasized by Uri Levine, Bret Taylor, Graham Weaver)

### Firing often works out for both sides
People are frequently relieved. The kindest path is often the hardest one. (Paul Adams, Julie Zhuo)

### Layoff execution
Cut deep once — multiple rounds create organizational PTSD. Calculate savings in dollars not headcount. Complete all notifications in a single morning. (Matt Mochary, Marc Benioff)

## 4. Peer Conflict & Difficult Emotions

### Stay on your side of the net
"Stick with the two realities you know — your intent and their behavior." Don't assume their motives. (Carole Robin)

### Address pinches before they become crunches
Small irritations escalate when ignored. Notice when you're becoming activated and speak up early. (Carole Robin)

### Feelings vs. attributions
"I feel that you don't care" is an attribution, not a feeling. Use actual emotion words: hurt, afraid, disappointed. The formula: "When you [behavior], I feel [feeling], and I'm telling you this because [purpose]." (Carole Robin, Kenneth Berger)

### "What did you hear me say?"
When someone reacts unexpectedly, check for misaligned perceptions. Validate their reaction based on what they heard, even if it wasn't what you intended. (Carole Robin)

### Approach disagreement with curiosity
"Fascinating. Tell me more about why you think that." (Boz, referencing a colleague's technique)

### Schedule conflict proactively — "Fight Club"
A dedicated 30-minute weekly slot for leadership conflict resolution. Enter knowing disagreements will surface. Prevents tensions from escalating. (Megan Cook)

### Take 100% responsibility for your contribution
"There is always something we're doing to contribute to the conflict." Enter with humility and curiosity. Ask: "How am I complicit in creating the conditions I claim I don't want?" (Rachel Lockett)

### Use "nevertheless" to close debate
"I hear you and understand. Nevertheless, this is what's going to happen." Validates the perspective, then transitions to the final decision. (Annie Duke)

### Use non-judgmental "I" statements for emotional de-escalation
"I perceive you to be in anger." No judgment — just a perception. Can punch through emotional hijacking and help someone pause. (Matt Mochary)

### Name the emotional shift in real time
"Let's pause — I can see the energy has changed. I have no intention of upsetting you. I just want to talk about things that will help your career." (Alisa Cohn)

### If someone looks sad after feedback
Pause and ask: "I feel like I didn't say that in the best way. How could I have said it differently?" Go up on care without retracting the challenge. (Kim Scott)

### Identify the emotion you're avoiding
Conflict avoidance is an attempt to avoid a specific feeling (out of control, ashamed). Practice tolerating that feeling to remove the resistance. (Joe Hudson, Drew Houston)

## 5. Delivering Bad News

### Provide hope and a path forward
"I know this is hard to hear — we're not going to promote you. But I'm committed to your success here, and here's what I'd like to do next." (Alisa Cohn)

### Don't bury the lead
Deliver the news early in the meeting. Explain the business rationale. Mirror back their perspective to show they've been heard. (Alisa Cohn)

> "We're going to be looking for an external candidate. We need someone who has done this role multiple times."

### Give a brief warning to prevent amygdala hijack
"This is going to be a difficult conversation. Take a few seconds to prepare yourself." Removing surprise reduces emotional flooding. (Matt Mochary)

### Own mistakes immediately in crisis
Take full responsibility without deflecting. Establish a proactive update cadence. Meet face-to-face as soon as possible. (Ethan Evans)

> "The first thing I did was own it. 'Yes, it's not working. It's my fault. I will deal with it.'"

### Reframe difficult conversations as opportunity
"On the other side of that upset can be a whole new possibility, revelation, and actually a lot of joy and freedom." (Alisa Cohn)

## 6. Receiving Feedback

### Respond with enthusiastic gratitude
Lower the social risk for the person giving you feedback. "Thank you — I mean it." This encourages future honesty. (Jules Walter)

### Ask for feedback on specific traits
Ask about a targeted area (e.g., "executive presence") rather than general performance. Offer self-critique first to make it safe. (Jules Walter, Mihika Kapoor)

### Let yourself feel before you analyze
Wait hours or days before responding. Don't react. Then ask: "Where is this coming from? Why am I getting this?" (Anneka Gupta)

### Listen fully without defending
When Katie Dill's team confronted her with a list of complaints, she had the sense to just listen and not respond defensively. That restraint was the turning point.

### Journal on the unsaid
"What am I not saying that I need to say? What am I saying that's not being heard? What's being said that I'm not hearing?" (Jerry Colonna)

### Model vulnerability publicly
Dedicate offsite time to "feedback to the leader" sessions. Acknowledge blind spots publicly to build a culture of honest feedback. (Varun Parmar)

### The AAA Apology (Matt Abrahams)
Acknowledge what you did. Appreciate the impact on them. Amend with a specific commitment to change.

### Say the thing you think you cannot say
Voice the observations you're tempted to filter out. Your biggest strength is often invisible to you. (Claire Hughes Johnson)
